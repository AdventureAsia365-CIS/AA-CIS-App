# The Sanmani Trail: 10 days walking the paths Korea's mountain foragers left behind

Most walking trails in Korea are built for recreation. The Sanmani Trail wasn't built at all. It was walked into existence — by foragers, refugees, and traders who used Jirisan's foothills because the mountain kept them alive.

For centuries, the Sanmani — traditional mountain foragers — moved between villages along Jirisan's lower slopes, harvesting wild ginseng, medicinal herbs, and forest products. During the Japanese invasions of the sixteenth century and again during the Korean War, civilians fled into these same valleys. The paths connect survival to commerce to daily life. Walking them now, you're not conquering a peak. You're tracing a living edge where people and mountain have depended on each other for generations.

I've led 12 departures on this trail since 2022. Here's what it looks like, day by day.

## Why the foothills, not the summit

Jirisan's summit — Cheonwangbong, 1,915 metres — is a different kind of walk. It's steep, exposed, and draws serious hikers who want altitude. The Dulegil circuit deliberately avoids it. Instead, the route follows the mountain's outer ring: village lanes, forest paths, farm tracks, river crossings, and the kind of terrain that reveals its character slowly rather than all at once.

The walking is real — 8 to 20 kilometres per day, with one day topping 1,000 metres of elevation gain. But the pace is village pace, not mountain pace. You stop when there's something to see. You eat when the village eats. The trail ends at Hwagae Market, where the mountain paths meet people and trade and conversation — exactly where the Sanmani ended their journeys too.

## Day 1 — Seoul's city wall, then south

The journey begins on the Hanyangdoseong — Seoul's historic city wall — with a 13-kilometre orientation walk through Inwangsan and Bugaksan. It's a considered start: walking together as a group for the first time, on terrain that connects the capital's urban present to its defended past. The views over Seoul are wide and unrestricted.

The welcome dinner includes a full briefing on the Sanmani Trail — its history, its daily shape, and what to expect physically. This isn't a walk you should start without understanding why the route exists.

## Day 2 — KTX south, first steps on the trail

The KTX carries the group from Seoul to Namwon in two and a half hours. After lunch, a vehicle transfer reaches the Unbong area — the starting point. The afternoon walk is gentle: 8 kilometres, 127 metres of elevation, along foothill paths through farmland and quiet villages. It's deliberately unhurried. The transition from city to mountain is the point.

## Day 3 — Namwon to Jucheon village (16km, +349m)

One of the most representative sections of the Dulegil. The path follows old village-to-village connections across open plains, rice fields, and gentle forest edges. These are paths shaped by necessity — farmers carrying produce to market, families visiting neighbours — not by trail designers. The walking is rhythmic. Jirisan's northern slopes are visible but distant. The day ends in Jucheon village.

## Day 4 — Jucheon to Banggwang village (14km, +526m)

The trail climbs through forest tracks and old mountain roads toward Gurijae Pass — the high point of the day and a traditional crossing between two watersheds. The ascent is steady, not steep. The pass itself is a place of transition: the vegetation changes, the air cools, and the valley you've been walking opens behind you. The descent into Banggwang village passes stone walls and small agricultural fields.

## Day 5 — Banggwang to Omi village (10km, +343m)

A gentler stage. The path traces foothill terrain through shaded forest and village roads. As you approach Omi, the landscape shifts — traditional timber-frame houses and tiled roofs appear. Omi sits within one of the most culturally intact rural areas of the Jirisan region. The pace slows here naturally. There's no need to push.

## Day 6 — Omi to Songjeong village (17km, +1,030m)

The most physically demanding day. A series of rises and descents along old village lanes, pastoral tracks, and shaded woodland paths. Small streams and forest groves provide natural rest points, and you'll use them.

**Honest note:** 1,030 metres of cumulative elevation in 17 kilometres is substantial. It's not technical — no scrambling, no exposure — but it's sustained and it asks for effort. Every departure has guests who find this day hard. That's fine. The support vehicle can shorten the route if needed. Songjeong village at the end is calm and unhurried — a quiet reward for the day's work.

## Day 7 — Songjeong to Hwagae Market (15km, +636m)

The trail opens onto rolling countryside and riverside paths, leading to Hwagae Market — a working traditional marketplace where farmers, traders, and travellers have gathered for centuries at the meeting point of the Honam and Yeongnam regions.

This is the trail's natural destination. The Sanmani walked these same paths to reach this same market. Arriving on foot, after days of village-to-village walking, gives the market a weight that a day-trip by car can't replicate. The group continues to Gatan for the night.

## Day 8 — Hwagae to Wonbuchun village (10km, +100m)

A gentle stage after the market's bustle. The trail climbs away from the river flats into quieter foothills. Distant views of terraced tea fields and wooded slopes. Wonbuchun village sits among fields and low hills — a settled, quiet finish to a measured day.

## Day 9 — Wonbuchun to Hadong (20km, +925m)

The longest day, and the last on foot. Rural lanes and wooded trails lead through Daechuk Village before descending into Hadong-eup. The landscape opens — broad views of agricultural fields and distant hills. The walk closes the loop around Jirisan's outer foothills.

A vehicle transfer carries the group from Hadong to Gwangyang city for the farewell dinner. The conversation is different from Day 1. The trail has done its work.

## Day 10 — KTX back to Seoul

A short vehicle transfer to Yeosu station, then three hours by KTX back to Seoul. The trail that began on the capital's city walls ends quietly on the return to them.

## What makes this trail different from other Korean hikes

Most walking content about Korea focuses on Seoraksan, Bukhansan, or Hallasan — summit hikes with cable cars and day-trip logistics. The Sanmani Trail is a different kind of walking entirely. It's multi-day, village-to-village, and culturally rooted in ways that summit hikes aren't.

The closest comparison in Europe would be the Camino de Santiago — not in religious terms, but in shape: a long-distance walk that moves through inhabited landscapes, where the journey's meaning comes from the accumulated days rather than any single viewpoint.

## FAQ

**How long does it take to walk the Sanmani Trail?**
The full Dulegil circuit takes 10 days as we structure it — including a day in Seoul, KTX travel days, and 7 days of walking. Daily distances range from 8km to 20km.

**Is Jirisan worth visiting?**
Absolutely — it's Korea's first national park and its most culturally significant mountain. The Dulegil circuit shows a different side than the summit routes: working villages, traditional agriculture, and paths with centuries of human history.

**What's the best time to walk?**
March–June and September–November. Autumn (October–November) is spectacular — the foliage along the lower slopes is among the best in Korea. We avoid July–August (monsoon) and deep winter.

**How physically demanding is the trail?**
Moderate overall. Most days are 10–16km with 300–600m of elevation gain. Day 6 (17km, 1,030m gain) is genuinely hard. Day 9 (20km, 925m gain) is long. A reasonable level of walking fitness is expected.

**What is the Dulegil trail?**
Dulegil (둘레길) means "perimeter path" in Korean. The Jirisan Dulegil follows the mountain's foothills rather than climbing to its summit, connecting villages and markets along routes used for centuries by the Sanmani — traditional mountain foragers.
