# Korea by bike: 9 days from Seoul to Busan on the route we've ridden for 6 years

There's a moment on Day 4, somewhere between the pine forests east of Seoul and the first glimpse of the East Sea, where the trip changes. You stop thinking about the kilometres and start noticing where you are. The traffic is gone. The trail is yours. The air smells different — salt and pine instead of city.

That's the moment I ride for. I've led this route since 2020, refining it after every departure. The bones are the same — Seoul to Busan by bike — but the details shift with every season. A restaurant closes, a trail section improves, a guest discovers something I'd missed. This itinerary is alive.

Here's the current version, day by day, with the details you'd need to ride it yourself — or to understand what it would feel like to ride it with us.

## The shape of the ride

Nine days. Seoul to Busan. Roughly 15 to 55 kilometres per day on the bike, with one DMZ visit by vehicle and a KTX return on Day 8. The route follows South Korea's dedicated cycling infrastructure — the Four Rivers Bike Path and East Sea coastal roads — on Trek FX 2 hybrid bikes provided for the trip. A support vehicle carries luggage between stops.

You don't need to be a competitive cyclist. You do need to be comfortable riding 40–50 kilometres in a day on a mix of paved path and quiet road. If you ride a bike at home on weekends, you'll manage. If you haven't ridden in five years, train for a month before you come.

## Day 1 — Arrival and first ride through Seoul

The group meets at 4pm for a bike check and route briefing. The introductory ride is short and deliberate — through Myeongdong's side streets, past a temple courtyard, along the first stretch of the Han River. It's not about distance; it's about settling into the bike and the rhythm of Korean roads.

The day closes with a shared hot pot dinner. Korean hot pot on the first evening has become a quiet tradition — it's communal, warming, and gives the group something to do with their hands while they're still learning each other's names.

## Day 2 — DMZ in the morning, Han River at dusk

This is the day that shapes the rest of the trip. The morning is spent at the Demilitarized Zone — Freedom Bridge, the Third Tunnel of Aggression (seven metres below the Korean border), and Dora Observatory. The guide provides commentary on the division and what remains unresolved. It is not a tourist attraction. It is a place where a war hasn't ended.

Lunch is taken near the border. The group is usually quieter than usual.

The afternoon is a 35-kilometre ride along the Han River trail system, timed so the city lights come up across the water as you ride. The contrast — the DMZ in the morning, Seoul's skyline at dusk — is intentional. It's the emotional range of Korea compressed into a single day.

## Day 3 — Train into the countryside, glamping on the Bukhan River

A morning train carries the group out of Seoul. The riding starts on the Four Rivers Bike Path — 35 kilometres along the Bukhan River through forested roads and rural villages, finishing in Cheongpyeong. A short transfer brings you to Gapyeong Glamping Park.

The afternoon is unscheduled: poolside, in the tents, or exploring. Dinner is Korean BBQ, outdoors, under open sky. On several departures, local guests have joined the group for dinner — not arranged, just the natural hospitality of a place where visitors are still unusual enough to be interesting.

## Day 4 — Fifty kilometres through pine forest to the East Sea

The most sustained riding day. Fifty kilometres through pine-covered hills and river valleys before arriving in Gangneung on the East Sea. The terrain is varied — quiet roads, gentle climbs, one section where the forest closes in and you can hear nothing but your tyres and the birds.

Gangneung is where the trip's character shifts. You're on the coast now. Free time options include the Heo Gyun and Heo Nanseolheon Memorial Park, the coffee shops of Anmok Street (Gangneung has become Korea's coffee capital — the concentration of roasters and pourover spots is remarkable), or the long flat beach at Jeongdongjin.

Dinner is Soondubu — soft tofu stew. It's a Gangneung staple and it earns its reputation after fifty kilometres.

## Day 5 — Coastal ride north to Sokcho

Fifty-five kilometres along Korea's eastern seaboard. This is the day that makes the trip — the road runs close to the shoreline between fishing villages, low cliffs, and occasional coastal pagodas. The East Sea is on your right for most of the morning. The riding is undulating rather than steep, and the scenery changes every few kilometres.

Sokcho is reached by mid-afternoon. Abai Village — a settlement built by refugees from the North after the Korean War — is worth an hour. It's a small, quiet place where the history is personal rather than monumental. The cable car up into Seoraksan National Park is available for those wanting mountain views; Sinheungsa Temple is a short walk from the entrance.

**Honest note:** Days 4 and 5 back-to-back (50km + 55km) are the most demanding stretch. If your legs are struggling after Day 4, the support vehicle can cover part of Day 5's distance. No one has ever been judged for using it.

## Day 6 — Transfer south, then riding through Gyeongju

A vehicle transfer to Pohang on the southeastern coast. From there, 33 kilometres along the Hyeongsan River toward Gyeongju, followed by 26 kilometres through the city itself. Gyeongju was the capital of the Silla dynasty for nearly a millennium — the grass-covered royal burial mounds sit in the middle of the city without ceremony, between apartment blocks and convenience stores.

Hwangnidan-gil Street is where the city lives now — artisan cafés, independent shops, hanok conversions. After dark, a short ride to Cheomseongdae Observatory and Anapji Pond is one of the trip's quieter highlights. The pond reflects the lanterns. The tourists are gone. The city belongs to whoever stayed.

## Day 7 — Busan: river delta to the sea

A morning transfer to Busan. The riding starts at Dadaepo Beach — 15 kilometres along the Nakdong River, then a subway transfer at Minam Station to skip the heavier traffic, then the final 16 kilometres along the southern coastline to Haeundae Beach.

Arriving at Haeundae on a bike after a week of riding feels different from arriving by taxi. The sea is the same, but you earned it. The afternoon is open — the Gowooni Ecological Trail, a swim, or time on the sand. The evening brings Busan's fountain show or a river cruise.

## Day 8 — KTX to Seoul, farewell dinner

The KTX high-speed train covers the Seoul–Busan corridor in under three hours. Window seats show rice paddies and mountain ridgelines — the same landscape you crossed by bike, compressed into a two-hour film.

The afternoon in Seoul is free. A taekwondo session, Gangnam's Hallyu K-Star Road, or the covered markets near the hotel. The group reconvenes for a farewell dinner in the evening — the bookend to Day 1's hot pot. By now the group has a shared language. The dinner is usually louder.

## Day 9 — Departure

No scheduled activities. Seoul's hanok neighbourhoods, smaller temple courtyards, and covered markets are all close. Departure at your own pace.

## What's different about this route

Most Korea cycling tours follow the Four Rivers Bike Path straight from Seoul to Busan — roughly 600 kilometres on dedicated trail. It's a fine ride and well-maintained. But it misses the coast, the DMZ, Abai Village, and the emotional range that comes from alternating between river, mountain, and shoreline.

This route deliberately breaks from the Four Rivers path after Day 3, cutting east to the coast and then south through Gyeongju. It adds vehicle transfers on Days 6 and 7 to keep the daily distances manageable while covering more geography. The result is a ride that sees more of Korea in nine days than a straight path would in twelve.

Competitors like SpiceRoads and TEAMAX offer strong routes on the Four Rivers path. What they don't offer is the DMZ on Day 2, the East Sea coastal riding on Day 5, or the night ride through Gyeongju on Day 6. Those are the days people talk about when they get home.

## FAQ

**Can you cycle from Seoul to Busan?**
Yes — South Korea's Four Rivers Bike Path provides a dedicated, largely traffic-free route of roughly 600 kilometres. Our tour covers the journey in 9 days using a modified route that adds coastal and cultural stops. The riding is 15–55 km per day.

**How fit do I need to be?**
Regularly active. If you cycle on weekends at home, you'll be comfortable. Days 4 and 5 (50km and 55km) are the most demanding. The support vehicle is always available if you need a lift for part of a stage.

**Is the Four Rivers Bike Path paved?**
Yes — well-maintained asphalt throughout. The path was purpose-built for cycling and is largely separated from road traffic. Korea has invested seriously in cycling infrastructure.

**What bikes are provided?**
Trek FX 2 Gen 4 hybrid bikes, fitted to each rider on Day 1. Helmets are compulsory — bring your own or purchase in Seoul before departure.

**What's the best time to ride?**
April–May (spring, cherry blossom) and September–October (autumn colour, cooler temperatures). We don't run departures in July–August (monsoon season) or deep winter.
