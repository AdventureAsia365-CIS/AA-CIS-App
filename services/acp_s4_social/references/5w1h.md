# 5W1H (What → Why → Who → When → Where → How)

5W1H is a clarity framework. It answers the core questions a reader needs before they can understand, trust, or act on a message. It is useful for product introductions, landing pages, FAQs, explainers, and announcements.

## 5 persuasive 5W1H models

### 1. Complete 5W1H (landing page, introduction post)

Answer What, Why, Who, When, Where, and How in a logical order. Use when the audience needs full context.

### 2. FAQ-style 5W1H (long-form content)

Turn each question into a section. Useful for educational content, onboarding pages, and complex offers.

### 3. Inverted 5W1H (Why first)

Start with Why, then explain What and How. Use when the mission, pain point, or market shift is more powerful than the product itself.

### 4. 5W1H Landing Outline

Use the framework as a page structure: hero, problem, audience, process, timing, location/channel, CTA.

### 5. Short 5W1H (captions, stories)

Answer only the most necessary questions in compressed form. Useful for social captions and short announcements.

## 7 core techniques in 5W1H

### 1. Singular Answer Rule

Each W should have one clear answer. Do not overload one section with multiple messages.

### 2. Identity Labeling (Who)

Make the target reader explicit. “For founders who…” is stronger than “for everyone.”

### 3. Cost of Inaction (Why)

Explain why this matters now. If there is no cost to waiting, the message loses urgency.

### 4. Temporal Landmark (When)

Use clear timing: today, this quarter, after launch, before the deadline, during onboarding, etc.

### 5. Friction Reduction (How)

Make the next step easy. Explain the process simply so the reader does not imagine hidden difficulty.

### 6. Channel Specificity (Where)

Tell the reader where the action happens: website, form, inbox, app, store, event, call, or email.

### 7. Self-Referencing Trigger (Who)

Help the reader connect the message to themselves: their role, pain, goal, identity, or situation.

## 8-point checklist before publishing 5W1H

1. Is What clear?
2. Is Why compelling?
3. Is Who specific?
4. Is When concrete?
5. Is Where obvious?
6. Is How simple?
7. Is there one clear CTA?
8. Does the order fit the channel and audience?

## Mistakes to avoid

1. Answering too many things at once.
2. Making Who too broad.
3. Forgetting Why.
4. Explaining How with too many steps.
5. Leaving the CTA unclear.
6. Writing a factual explanation without persuasion.

## Recommended psychology

Cognitive Fluency, Cost of Inaction, Specificity Bias, Identity Labeling, and Friction Reduction.

## Recommended NLP

Question framing, presupposition, direct commands, and self-referencing language.
