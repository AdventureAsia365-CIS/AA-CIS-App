# ACC (Agreement → Credibility → Call to Action)

ACC begins by creating agreement, then builds credibility, then invites action. It is useful for trust-building content, expert posts, founder posts, educational posts, and softer selling.

## 5 persuasive ACC models

### 1. Classic ACC (brand-building and sharing posts)

Start with a statement the audience can agree with, support it with credibility, then close with a light CTA.

### 2. ACC Bookend

Open and close with agreement. This reinforces the shared belief and makes the CTA feel natural.

### 3. ACC with Story

Use a short story inside the credibility section. The story becomes proof rather than decoration.

### 4. ACC Authority Stack

Use layered proof: experience, data, case study, credential, or third-party validation. Good for experts and B2B.

### 5. ACC Commitment Ladder

Use a low-friction CTA first, then move the audience toward stronger commitment over time.

## 7 core techniques in ACC

### 1. Universal Agreement Opener

Begin with something the audience already believes or has experienced. This lowers resistance.

### 2. Foot-in-the-Door Trigger

Start with a small agreement so a bigger action later feels consistent.

### 3. Specificity Stack with Story

Make credibility concrete through details, numbers, people, timelines, and outcomes.

### 4. Halo Effect Link

Borrow trust from credible associations: recognized clients, institutions, partners, media, awards, or expertise.

### 5. Insight Hook

The credibility section should not only prove; it should teach one useful insight.

### 6. Soft CTA Trigger

Use a CTA that feels aligned with the content: save, comment, download, read, book, reply, or learn more.

### 7. Reverse Psychology

For resistant audiences, reduce pressure by making the action optional or selective. Example logic: “This is not for everyone; it is for…”

## 8-point checklist before publishing ACC

1. Does the opening create real agreement?
2. Is the credibility specific?
3. Is there a useful insight, not just self-promotion?
4. Does the proof fit the claim?
5. Is the CTA soft or strong enough for the audience stage?
6. Does the tone feel trustworthy?
7. Is the content free from exaggeration?
8. Does the ending reinforce the shared belief?

## Mistakes to avoid

1. Opening with a claim the audience may not agree with.
2. Using vague credibility.
3. Turning the post into bragging.
4. Using a hard CTA too early.
5. Missing the insight.

## Recommended psychology

Commitment and Consistency, Authority, Halo Effect, Social Proof, Cognitive Ease, and Trust Transfer.

## Recommended NLP

Agreement frames, presupposition, identity labeling, soft commands, and belief reinforcement.
