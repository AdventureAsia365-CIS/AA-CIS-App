# FAB (Features → Advantages → Benefits)

FAB translates what a product has into why it matters. A feature is what it is. An advantage is what it does better. A benefit is what the customer gains. FAB prevents content from becoming a list of specifications.

## 5 persuasive FAB models

### 1. Classic FAB (one main feature)

Structure: Feature → Advantage → Benefit.

Use for product descriptions, ecommerce listings, service explanations, and short ads.

### 2. FAB Matrix (three parallel features)

Use when the product has multiple important features. Present each feature with its advantage and benefit so the reader can compare value quickly.

### 3. Reverse ABF (Benefit first)

Structure: Benefit → Advantage → Feature.

Use when the emotional outcome is more important than the technical detail. Strong for social posts, landing page hero sections, and short ads.

### 4. Comparative FAB

Structure: Competitor/default feature → Your feature → Advantage → Customer benefit.

Use in crowded markets where customers need to understand what makes the offer different.

### 5. FAB Stack (B2B, multi-stakeholder)

Map features to benefits for different stakeholders: user, manager, buyer, executive, operations, finance. Useful for SaaS, consulting, and professional services.

## 7 core techniques in FAB

### 1. Feature Translation Rule

Never leave a feature alone. Translate it into an advantage, then into a benefit. Ask: “So what?” until the customer outcome is obvious.

### 2. Sensory Language (Benefit)

Make the benefit easy to imagine. Replace abstract outcomes with concrete scenes, feelings, or user experiences.

### 3. Endowment Effect Trigger

Write benefits as if the reader is already experiencing the improved state. This helps them mentally “own” the outcome.

### 4. Contrast Framing (Advantage)

Show the difference between the old way and the new way. Contrast makes the advantage easier to understand.

### 5. Benefit Hierarchy

Order benefits from practical to emotional, or from immediate to long-term. Put the strongest benefit at the end when possible.

### 6. Story Anchor

Attach the benefit to a small real-life scenario. A feature becomes memorable when the reader sees it in use.

### 7. Premium Power Words

For premium products, use precise, restrained language. Avoid loud hype. Let craftsmanship, ease, trust, privacy, reliability, and selectivity carry the value.

## 8-point checklist before publishing FAB

1. Is every feature translated into a customer advantage?
2. Is every advantage translated into a customer benefit?
3. Are benefits specific, not generic?
4. Is there contrast against the old way or common alternative?
5. Does the order of benefits build momentum?
6. Is the language appropriate for price and brand position?
7. Is there proof where the claim needs proof?
8. Is the CTA connected to the strongest benefit?

## Mistakes to avoid

1. Listing features without explaining why they matter.
2. Confusing advantage with benefit.
3. Using benefits that are too broad, such as “better life” or “save time” without detail.
4. Overloading the reader with too many features.
5. Making premium products sound cheap through exaggerated language.

## Recommended psychology

Specificity Bias, Endowment Effect, Contrast Effect, Authority, Social Proof, and Value Perception.

## Recommended NLP

“So you can…” translation, sensory language, future pacing, embedded commands, and precise benefit framing.
