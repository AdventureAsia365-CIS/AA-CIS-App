# AIDA (Attention → Interest → Desire → Action)

AIDA is one of the classic copywriting formulas. It guides the customer through four emotional states: from stopping their scroll, to becoming interested, to wanting the offer, to taking action. AIDA works because people do not move straight from “I saw an ad” to “I bought it.” They first need attention, then curiosity, then desire, then a clear next step. If any step is missing, the reader falls out of the funnel.

## 5 persuasive AIDA models

Choose the model based on content length and conversion goal.

### 1. Classic AIDA (medium ads, 200–400 words)

Balanced four-part structure: Attention (1–2 sentences) → Interest (2–3 sentences) → Desire (3–5 sentences) → Action (1–2 sentences).

Use for Facebook ads, medium-length sales posts, and email marketing. Best for mid-priced products where customers need enough information but do not need long persuasion. This is the default baseline when you are unsure which model to use.

### 2. AIDAS (adds Satisfaction)

Structure: Attention → Interest → Desire → Action → Satisfaction.

The “Satisfaction” step comes after Action. It reassures the reader about the experience after purchase: warranty, support, refund, trial, onboarding, or after-sale care. Use this for higher-priced products or categories where customers may doubt quality, such as beauty, supplements, online courses, or services. Satisfaction reduces the fear of “what if this is not what I expected?”

### 3. AIDCA (adds Conviction)

Structure: Attention → Interest → Desire → Conviction → Action.

Conviction sits between Desire and Action. This section proves the claim with numbers, testimonials, certifications, case studies, or expert authority. Use for B2B products, professional services, consulting, legal/accounting services, or bottom-of-funnel audiences that already know the offer and need final proof before converting.

### 4. AIDMA (adds Memory)

Structure: Attention → Interest → Desire → Memory → Action.

Memory reinforces the message with one image, slogan, key phrase, or memorable number. Use for brand awareness and brand-building content where conversion is not immediate. Example logic: repeat one simple line at the start and end so people remember the brand even if they do not buy today.

### 5. Long-form AIDA (VSL, 1,500+ words)

Extended structure: Attention (one hook + one reason to keep reading) → Interest (3–5 context-building paragraphs + insight) → Desire (detailed future pacing + customer story) → Action (offer + scarcity + risk reversal + CTA).

Use for video sales letters, course sales pages, long landing pages, or high-ticket offers. Each AIDA stage becomes several mini-sections. This is the “heavy artillery” version of AIDA, useful when the price is high or the audience needs end-to-end persuasion.

## 7 core techniques in AIDA

Use at least four of these seven techniques in any AIDA piece.

### 1. Pattern Interrupt Hook (Attention)

The first line must break the reader’s scrolling pattern. Three strong hook types are: a surprising statistic that contradicts common belief, a sharp question aimed at a specific pain, or a contrarian statement that creates cognitive tension. The reader stops because the brain detects an “error” and wants to resolve it.

### 2. Curiosity Gap (Attention → Interest)

Open an information gap the reader wants to close. Example logic: “There are three mistakes most beginners make. Mistake #2 is the one people rarely notice.” Curiosity moves the reader from “I saw this” to “I need to know this.” Avoid empty clickbait; the answer must deliver real value.

### 3. Sensory Language (Interest)

Describe the product or outcome through sight, sound, touch, taste, or feeling. Sensory language is stronger than abstract claims because the brain processes it emotionally. Each Interest section should include at least one concrete or sensory detail.

### 4. Future Pacing with Embedded Command (Desire)

Paint a specific picture of life after the product. Use the structure: “Imagine…” + sensory detail + desired emotion. The instruction to imagine makes the reader mentally simulate the result, which activates a more emotional form of desire.

### 5. Dopamine Ladder (Desire)

List three to five benefits in rising emotional intensity. Start with a small concrete benefit, move to a larger life improvement, then end with the most emotionally meaningful outcome. The reader climbs the benefit ladder and feels momentum.

### 6. Triple Trigger Action (Action)

A strong CTA contains three parts: a specific action verb, a time-bound reason to act, and subtle loss aversion. Example logic: “Message us before midnight to keep your early-access slot.” The action is clear, the timing is clear, and the cost of waiting is implied.

### 7. Double Bind Close (Action)

Offer two choices, both leading toward conversion. Instead of asking “Do you want to buy?”, ask which option they prefer. This shifts the decision from yes/no to option selection. Use carefully and ethically, especially for ecommerce or packages with multiple choices.

## 8-point checklist before publishing AIDA

1. Does the first line make a scrolling reader pause within two seconds?
2. Does the Interest section include at least one sensory detail or specific data point?
3. Does the Desire section use clear future pacing rather than only listing features?
4. Are there at least two psychological triggers such as loss aversion, scarcity, social proof, or FOMO?
5. Does the CTA include action verb + urgency + loss aversion?
6. Are paragraphs short, readable, and rhythmic?
7. Does the tone match the requested brand voice?
8. Is there one clear main message, without dilution?

If three or more items are missing, rewrite.

## Mistakes to avoid

1. Weak Attention: opening with generic greetings or “Have you heard of product X?”
2. Feature-heavy Interest: listing specs without translating them into customer meaning.
3. Vague Desire: saying “you will be happier” without a specific scene, time, character, or action.
4. Weak Action: using generic CTAs such as “Contact us” or “Click the link” without clear value.
5. Unbalanced stages: making Attention too long or Action too short. A useful ratio for classic AIDA is 1:2:3:1.
6. No proof: making claims without numbers, testimonials, certifications, or credible evidence.

## Recommended psychology

Pattern Interrupt + Curiosity Gap for Attention; Authority + Specificity Bias for Interest; Future Pacing + Endowment Effect + Sensory Anchor for Desire; Scarcity + Loss Aversion + FOMO + Social Proof for Action; Risk Reversal for AIDAS.

## Recommended NLP

Open Loop or Presupposition in the opening; Sensory Language through Interest and Desire; Embedded Commands in Desire; Double Bind in Action; emotional power words throughout; specific numbers and time markers to activate specificity bias.
