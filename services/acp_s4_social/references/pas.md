# PAS (Problem → Agitate → Solution)

PAS is a direct-response framework that begins with a problem the audience recognizes, intensifies the cost of leaving it unsolved, then presents the solution. It works best when the customer already feels pain or dissatisfaction but has not yet committed to a solution.

## 5 persuasive PAS models

### 1. Classic PAS (audience knows the problem)

Structure: Problem → Agitate → Solution.

Use for warm audiences, retargeting, pain-aware social posts, and ads. The key is to name the problem in the customer’s own language, not in the brand’s language.

### 2. PASTOR (expanded PAS)

Structure: Problem → Amplify → Story/Solution → Transformation → Offer → Response.

Use for longer sales pages, webinar emails, courses, consulting, and high-trust services. It gives more room for proof, story, offer details, and risk reversal.

### 3. PAS-Loop (cold audience)

Structure: Hidden Problem → Symptom → Agitate → Reveal Root Cause → Solution.

Use when the audience does not yet realize they have the problem. Start with a visible symptom, then reveal the deeper issue.

### 4. PAS-Reverse (solution first)

Structure: Solution Hook → Problem Reveal → Agitation → CTA.

Use when the solution itself is surprising or curiosity-driving. This works well for short-form social posts and product demos.

### 5. PAS-Stack (B2B or premium services)

Structure: Business Problem → Operational Cost → Strategic Risk → Solution → Proof → Next Step.

Use for B2B, consulting, SaaS, professional services, or high-value offers where multiple stakeholders need rational reasons to act.

## 7 core techniques in PAS

### 1. Customer Voice Hook (Problem)

Use language the customer would actually say. A problem written in customer language feels recognized; a problem written in brand language feels like marketing.

### 2. Pain Amplification (Agitate)

Agitation should deepen understanding, not create cheap fear. Show the emotional, financial, time, social, or operational cost of the problem.

### 3. Cost of Inaction (Agitate)

Make the cost of doing nothing visible. The reader must understand that avoiding a decision is still a decision with consequences.

### 4. Identifiable Victim (Agitate)

Use one specific person, situation, or scenario rather than a vague crowd. Specificity makes the pain feel real.

### 5. Solution Bridge

Do not jump suddenly from problem to product. Use a bridge such as: “The real issue is not X. It is Y. That is why the solution needs to…”

### 6. Risk Reversal (Solution)

Reduce fear through guarantee, trial, transparent process, support, refund, demo, consultation, or clear next steps.

### 7. Specificity Stack (Solution)

Use numbers, timelines, steps, proof, screenshots, credentials, or case studies. Specificity makes the solution more believable.

## 8-point checklist before publishing PAS

1. Is the problem specific and written in customer language?
2. Does agitation explain real cost without becoming manipulative?
3. Is the root cause clear?
4. Does the solution feel like a natural answer to the problem?
5. Is there proof or specificity?
6. Is fear balanced with hope?
7. Is the CTA clear and low-friction?
8. Does the content avoid exaggeration or unsupported claims?

## Mistakes to avoid

1. Naming a problem the audience does not care about.
2. Over-agitating until the content feels negative or manipulative.
3. Presenting the product too early.
4. Making the solution vague.
5. Forgetting proof.
6. Using generic pain words instead of specific scenarios.

## Recommended psychology

Loss Aversion, Cost of Inaction, Specificity Bias, Social Proof, Authority, Risk Reversal, and Relief Framing.

## Recommended NLP

Customer-language mirroring, Open Loops, Presupposition, Embedded Commands, Sensory Contrast, and future pacing after the solution.
