# PPPP (Problem → Promise → Proof → Proposal)

PPPP is a strong sales and landing-page framework. It names the problem, makes a promise, proves the promise, then presents the proposal or offer. It works well when the audience needs both emotional and rational confidence.

## 5 persuasive PPPP models

### 1. Classic PPPP

Use for sales posts and standard landing pages: Problem → Promise → Proof → Proposal.

### 2. PPPP with Case Study

Make the proof section a detailed case study. Useful for B2B, consulting, courses, and high-value services.

### 3. PPPP Comparative

Compare the offer against the common alternative or competitor. Keep comparison specific and fair.

### 4. PPPP Pricing Tier

Use for pricing pages with multiple packages. Problem and promise remain shared; proposal becomes tiered.

### 5. Long-form PPPP Sales Page

Expand each section into multiple subsections. Useful for VSLs and 2,000+ word sales pages.

## 7 core techniques in PPPP

### 1. Cost of Inaction Quantification (Problem)

Make the problem measurable when possible: money lost, hours wasted, opportunities missed, risk increased.

### 2. Specific Promise with Deadline

A promise is stronger when it includes a specific outcome, mechanism, and time horizon.

### 3. Three-Tier Proof Stack

Use proof at three levels: data, testimonial/case, and authority/credential.

### 4. Mechanism Explanation

Explain how the promise becomes possible. A clear mechanism increases believability.

### 5. Halo Effect Chain

Use credible associations to strengthen trust: partners, clients, media, awards, experts, or institutions.

### 6. Decoy Pricing

When appropriate, structure packages so the best-value option is easy to identify.

### 7. Risk Reversal Stacking

Combine guarantee, trial, support, transparent process, or clear cancellation to reduce fear.

## 8-point checklist before publishing PPPP

1. Is the problem urgent and specific?
2. Is the promise clear and believable?
3. Is the mechanism explained?
4. Is the proof strong enough for the claim?
5. Is the proposal easy to understand?
6. Is pricing or next step clear?
7. Is risk reduced?
8. Is there one clear CTA?

## Mistakes to avoid

1. Big promise with weak proof.
2. Problem that is too broad.
3. Proposal that is confusing.
4. Pricing without value framing.
5. Missing mechanism.
6. Unsupported guarantees.

## Recommended psychology

Cost of Inaction, Authority, Social Proof, Anchoring, Risk Reversal, Scarcity, and Specificity Bias.

## Recommended NLP

Presupposition, future pacing, specific numbers, contrast framing, embedded commands, and certainty language.
